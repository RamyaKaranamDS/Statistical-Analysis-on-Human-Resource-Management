Interpretation:¶

 Grip and arm strength are positively related, meaning individuals with strong grips also tend to have strong arms.

Grip strength shows a moderate positive connection with success in simulated physically demanding tasks (SIM scores). Those with stronger grips often perform better in simulations.

However, the relationship between grip strength and subjective job performance ratings is weak, suggesting that having a strong grip alone may not strongly influence how supervisors rate job performance.

Similarly, while there's a moderate positive connection between arm strength and SIM scores, the correlation between arm strength and job performance ratings is also weak.

Conclusion :¶

The correlation analysis suggests that while physical strength, especially grip and arm strength, is positively associated with certain aspects of job performance (simulations), the relationship is not extremely strong with subjective job performance ratings.

Other factors beyond physical strength may also play a role in determining job performance ratings.

from scipy.stats import pearsonr

alpha = 0.05

# Hypothesis 1: Correlation between grip strength and simulation scores
corr_GRIP_SIMS, p_value_GRIP_SIMS = pearsonr(df['GRIP'], df['SIMS'])
print(f"Pearson correlation coefficient (GRIP vs. SIMS): {corr_GRIP_SIMS}, p-value: {p_value_GRIP_SIMS}")

# Hypothesis 2: Correlation between arm strength and simulation scores
corr_ARM_SIMS, p_value_ARM_SIMS = pearsonr(df['ARM'], df['SIMS'])
print(f"Pearson correlation coefficient (ARM vs. SIMS): {corr_ARM_SIMS}, p-value: {p_value_ARM_SIMS}")

# Hypothesis 3: Correlation between supervisor ratings and simulation scores
corr_RATINGS_SIMS, p_value_RATINGS_SIMS = pearsonr(df['RATINGS'], df['SIMS'])
print(f"Pearson correlation coefficient (RATINGS vs. SIMS): {corr_RATINGS_SIMS}, p-value: {p_value_RATINGS_SIMS}")

Conclusion :¶

p-value is less than the chosen significance level ( 0.05), so we reject the null hypothesis.

For both grip strength and arm strength, there is strong evidence of a positive correlation with simulation scores.

Supervisor ratings also show a positive correlation with simulation scores, although it is weaker compared to the correlations with physical strength measures.

These results provide statistical evidence supporting the relationships between physical strength measures and simulation scores. The small p-values suggest that these correlations are unlikely to be due to random chance.

Recommendations:¶

When selecting candidates for physically demanding jobs, considerations beyond physical strength, 
such as skills and experience, should also be taken into account.

Simulation tasks may provide a more reliable measure of a candidate's 
ability to perform in a physically demanding role compared to subjective ratings alone.
